"For humans to survive on other worlds, they’ll have to harness the resources – like water – that exist there."

–NASA

NASA aspires for human exploration of Mars in the 2030s, and robotic missions have helped gather information about Mars' environment, available resources, and how to allow sustained human presence on the surface. Among the resources explored is water: where it is located, what is its purity, and how to obtain it in large quantities. As it serves as one of the major determining factors to whether missions to Mars are possible, sourcing water is a rich area of research.

The Carnegie Mellon University Tartan Ice Miners believe we can apply mining techniques to successfully extract Martian ice with minimal overburden interference and loss of water to sublimation. Our proposed system, the Tartan Ice Drilling System (TIDS), utilizes a double-barreled coring drill to provide the highest amount of water extraction with lowest energy exerted. Once extracted, the ice cores will be melted in a heating chamber and filtered through vaporization. A low-power microcontroller will allow real-time feedback and monitoring of the system, as well as remote intervention if necessary.


Our team is comprised of undergraduate students from a variety of disciplines and backgrounds with a shared passion for space research. Carnegie Mellon University stresses the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration, and we are excited to combine our individual experiences and expertise to create a successful ice drilling mechanism.
